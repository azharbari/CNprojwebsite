Unit 1, 1.1 Describing the purpose of IT in modern business

These days IT touches every single industry and business you can think of no matter how big or small. Some of the things modern businesses use IT for include advertising, securing, training employees, storing data, receiving and making payments. You as a consumer on a weekly basis may use the internet for purchasing something, ordering a takeaway delivery, paying bills and even hailing a taxi.

For example let's take a restaurant to describe the purpose of IT in modern business. The restaurant will need to advertise its business so they can broaden their audience. They can provide the audience with information such as their location, contact details, opening closing times and even allow them to make a seat reservation online. They can also extend this to creating an option for their audience to make an order online for delivery, taking payment for this and also providing feedback to promote the restaurants goodwill even further. The restaurant can also use IT to train the staff in food hygiene for example by getting them to complete an online course. It will also need to submit a tax return to HMRC which they may also do online and they may pay all the other bills online too. Therefore it can be seen that the purpose of IT in modern business is an integral and vital part to it and can make life more convenient for owners, employees and consumers a like.

1.1.7 Describe legal guidelines and constraints that impact digital projects

The General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) is a legal framework that sets guidelines for the collection and processing of personal information from individuals who live in the EU. [1]   In particular it permanently changed the way you can collect, store and use data. Thus it can impact digital projects in that they would need to put in to place safeguards against the breaking of the rules set out by the GDPR as the punishments can be severe. The digital projects would need to ensure that the data collected can be identified as to where it resides, who can access it and if there are any risks to the data. Furthermore it would need to made sure that irrelevant data for the purposes of the project are deleted and discarded as GDPR requires data held to be relevant to the projects purposes. Also the data would need to be kept secure by putting security measures in place to guard against data breaches, and taking quick action to notify individuals and authorities in the event a breach does occur [2].

2.1.5 Describing copyright and other constraints affecting websites

A website and the things within a website are creations and like any creation they can be someone else’s intellectual property. Therefore we need to be very careful that the images or documents we use in the project are not a protected intellectual property by the owner. Ways in which an owner protects their intellectual property are Copyright,Trademarks, Patents, Design rights and Resisted designs[3]. It can be seen that copyright protection is automatic when original written work such as books, articles and web content or software.  The copyright symbol is not needed to protect it, but one can include it as a reminder to others that they own the IP[3]. However there are a few permitted circumstances where permission is not required for the use of copyrighted works. These include non-commercial research and private study, teaching and helping disabled people[4]. Along with copyright websites also need to consider the impact of the GDPR affecting websites by ensuring that the data collected for the website does not publish an identifiable individual without the individual’s consent. Therefore websites need to consider the data they collect and publish on to their website with extreme care to avoid breaking the law. Ways in which they can do this is seeing if there is an explicit requirement of consent on the document, obtain the consent of individuals before publishing their personal data, seeing if there is a Trademark or even a watermark covering the document, or seeking professional advice.

2.1.6 Describing access issues that need taking into account

We would need to take in to account some accessibility issues that people with disabilities may encounter when accessing a webpage. Some of these disabilities may include auditory, cognitive, neurological, physical and visual impairments[5]. These disabilities can hinder or even prevent an individual from using and experiencing the webpage. Some of the ways in which we can overcome these barriers to access are providing alternatives to images in the form of text in the markup code. This allows the information to be available to people who are blind (with screen reading out loud tech), as well as to people who turn off images. It’s also available to technologies that cannot see images, such as search engines. The need to make sure the colours that are selected on the website contrast well to ensure that everyone can distinguish between various elements on the page. This can be done for example by choosing a dark colour for text on top of light background so the text can be easily read and distinguished.[6]. For those who cannot use a mouse for any reason a website can be made accessible using assistive technologies that mimic the keyboard such as speech input [5]. For those who are deaf or hard of hearing transcripts can be provided on the webpage for any audio on it[5]. These methods combined can make a webpage more accessible and enable it to reach everyone who visits it.

2.1.7 Describe which are the appropriate filetypes for websites

There are many different filetypes which can be put on to a website including different kinds of documents such as text and spreadsheets, images, audio and video. We need to be careful not to overload the website with a lot of files as it would cause a lag in the time it takes for it to load. HTML files are an appropriate filetype for a website because this file format forms the building blocks of the web. HTML is also a language which marks up a website in a plain text editor which then appears in a browser in the form of what should be a fully functional website. Appropriate image filetypes for the website are JPEG and PNG files as they are low in memory and load quite quickly to illustrate the website in the form of images. Video filetypes can be inappropriate as they can cause a lag in the website to load as they can be quite high in memory. An alternative to video files could be a GIF file to illustrate a short clip of what is trying to be shown as they are quite low in memory and can load quickly. It might be suggested that if a webpage does require a video file it should keep these down to the minimum and absolute necessary.
